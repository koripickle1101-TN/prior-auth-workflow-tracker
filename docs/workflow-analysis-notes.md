# Workflow analysis notes

## Operational problem

Prior authorization delays often start with incomplete documentation, unclear ownership, payer-specific requirements, or weak follow-up routines.

These problems can create:

- delayed scheduling
- staff rework
- patient frustration
- avoidable denials
- unclear accountability
- manual status chasing

## Workflow sequence

1. Service ordered
2. Authorization requirement checked
3. Documentation gathered
4. Request submitted
5. Payer status monitored
6. Missing items corrected
7. Escalation completed when needed
8. Authorization decision documented
9. Scheduling or billing handoff completed

## Key handoff risks

Clinical to authorization handoff

Risk:

The authorization team receives the request without enough clinical support.

Authorization to payer handoff

Risk:

The payer receives an incomplete or incorrectly categorized request.

Payer to internal team handoff

Risk:

A payer response is missed, delayed, or not routed to the correct owner.

Authorization to scheduling handoff

Risk:

Approval is received but the patient access step is not completed.

## Trust signals

Timing trust

Teams trust that status updates are current.

Routing trust

Teams trust that the request is assigned to the correct owner.

Context trust

Teams trust that the request contains the needed clinical and payer information.

Reconciliation trust

Teams trust that approval, denial, or missing-item outcomes are tracked and closed.
